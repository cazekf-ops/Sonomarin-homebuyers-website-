Do You Have to Go Through Probate?

Not always — it depends on how the property was held:

  • Held in a living trust, joint tenancy, or with a transfer-on-death deed — you can often sell without full probate.
  • Owned solely by the person who passed — if the estate is above California's small-estate threshold (about $184,500), probate is generally required before the home can be sold.

Probate sounds intimidating, but it's a well-worn process, and you don't have to navigate it alone. Your Options for an Inherited Property

There's no single right answer — the best path depends on your family's goals:

  • Move in or keep it — if the home works for you and the numbers make sense.
  • Rent it out — for ongoing income, if you're prepared to be a landlord.
  • Sell it — the most common choice, especially with multiple heirs or an out-of-area executor. With time, a traditional listing may net the most; when you want speed and certainty, a cash sale is simplest.

The Tax Angle: Step-Up in Basis

Here's a piece of good news many people don't realize: inherited property usually receives a stepped-up cost basis — its value resets to the fair market value on the date of the previous owner's death. That means if you sell soon after inheriting, your taxable capital gain is often small or even zero, because you're only taxed on appreciation since that date. Every situation is different, so confirm the details with a tax professional — but for many families this makes selling far less costly than they feared.

Common Questions

Inherited & Probate FAQs — Sonoma & Marin County

Do I have to go through probate to sell an inherited house in California?

It depends on how the property was held. If the home was in a living trust, held in joint tenancy, or passed by a transfer-on-death deed, you can often sell without full probate. If it was owned solely by the person who passed and the estate exceeds California's small-estate threshold (about $184,500), probate is usually required before selling.

Can I sell an inherited house that still has a mortgage?

Yes. The mortgage is simply paid off from the sale proceeds at closing, just like any other sale. If the home has equity, the remaining balance goes to the estate or heirs. We handle the payoff coordination as part of the closing.

Do all the heirs have to agree to sell the inherited property?

Generally, whoever has legal authority to sell — the executor or administrator, or all co-owners on title — must agree. When several heirs share ownership, it's usually smoothest when everyone is aligned. A clear, fair cash offer on the table often helps families reach agreement faster.

Will I owe a lot in taxes if I sell an inherited house?

Often less than people expect. Inherited property usually receives a stepped-up cost basis to its fair market value at the date of death, so if you sell soon after, your taxable gain may be small or zero. Everyone's situation differs, so confirm the specifics with a tax professional.

The inherited house needs a lot of work — do I have to fix it up?

No. We buy inherited homes exactly as they are, including houses with decades of deferred maintenance or full of belongings. You don't need to clean, repair, or clear anything out — we handle all of it after closing.

How fast can you close on an inherited property?

Once any required probate authority is in place and the title is clear, we can close in as little as 7 days — or on whatever timeline works for the family. If the estate is still in probate, we're patient and familiar with the court's timeline.
