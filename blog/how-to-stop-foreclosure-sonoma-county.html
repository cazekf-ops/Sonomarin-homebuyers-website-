How to Stop Foreclosure in California — Your Options Before the Auction

By Evan & Alex, Sonomarin Home Buyers  |  Serving Sonoma & Marin County, CA

If you're behind on your mortgage or you've received a Notice of Default, the fear can be paralyzing. But foreclosure in California is a defined legal process — not something that happens overnight — and at almost every stage you have options. This guide walks through exactly how foreclosure works in California, what your rights are, every way to stop it, and why selling before the auction is so often the move that protects you most.

First, Take a Breath: Foreclosure Is a Process, Not an Instant Event

The single most important thing to understand is that you almost certainly have more time than it feels like. From the moment your lender records the first official notice, California law builds in waiting periods measured in months, not days. During that entire window, you still own your home, and you keep the right to sell it, reinstate the loan, or work out an alternative with your lender. The home isn't gone until the trustee's sale actually happens.

Judicial vs. Non-Judicial Foreclosure in California

Nearly all California foreclosures are non-judicial. When you took out your loan, you signed a deed of trust containing a "power of sale" clause, which lets the lender foreclose through a trustee without going to court. It's faster than the judicial route and follows the timeline below. Judicial foreclosure — where the lender sues in court — is rare in California and mostly used when a lender wants to preserve the right to pursue a deficiency. For the vast majority of Sonoma and Marin homeowners, the non-judicial timeline is what applies.

The California Foreclosure Timeline, Step by Step

1. Missed payments and default. After you miss a payment you'll see late fees and notices. Under federal rules, your servicer generally cannot start the formal foreclosure process until you're more than 120 days delinquent. This early stretch is the best time to act, because you have the most options and the most leverage.

2. Notice of Default (NOD). The lender (through the trustee) records a Notice of Default with the county recorder — the Sonoma County Clerk-Recorder in Santa Rosa, or the Marin County Recorder in San Rafael — and mails you a copy. This is public record, and it starts the official clock. From here, the lender must wait a minimum of 90 days before taking the next step.

3. The reinstatement window. During that 90-day period — and beyond it — you generally have the right to "reinstate" the loan by paying the past-due amount plus allowable fees, up until five business days before the sale. You can also sell the home during this entire window.

4. Notice of Trustee's Sale (NOS). If the default isn't cured, after the 90 days the trustee records and publishes a Notice of Trustee's Sale, which sets the auction date at least 21 days out. It's published in a local paper, posted on the property, and mailed to you.

5. The trustee's sale (auction). Your home is sold to the highest bidder, often the lender itself via a credit bid. In a non-judicial foreclosure there is generally no right of redemption afterward — meaning once the sale happens, you can't buy the home back. That makes the sale date your true deadline.

Add it up and the minimum is roughly 111 days from the Notice of Default to the auction (90 + 21) — and in real life it's frequently much longer, sometimes 200+ days from your first missed payment. The point: there is almost always a window to act.

This guide is general information, not legal advice. Foreclosure situations are specific, and timelines and options can vary. A HUD-approved housing counselor can review your situation for free, and a California real estate attorney can advise on your particular case. We're glad to point you toward those resources.

Your Rights as a Homeowner in Foreclosure

California gives homeowners several meaningful protections during this process:

  • The right to reinstate. You can typically catch the loan up (pay the arrears plus fees) until five business days before the sale.
  • Protection from "dual tracking." Under the California Homeowner Bill of Rights, your servicer generally can't push forward with a foreclosure sale while a complete application for a loan modification is pending.
  • A single point of contact. Larger servicers must give you a consistent contact person to work with on alternatives to foreclosure.
  • Anti-deficiency protection. After a non-judicial trustee's sale in California, lenders generally cannot come after you for the remaining shortfall on most home loans — though you should confirm your specifics with an attorney.

Every Option to Stop Foreclosure — and When Each Makes Sense

There's no single right answer; it depends on whether keeping the home is realistic. Here's the full menu:

If you want to keep the home

  • Reinstate the loan — pay the past-due balance in a lump sum. Best when a temporary hardship has passed.
  • Repayment plan or forbearance — your servicer spreads the missed payments over time, or pauses them briefly. Good for short-term setbacks.
  • Loan modification — the lender permanently changes your terms (rate, length, or balance) to make payments affordable. The right path if your income can support a reworked payment.
  • Refinance — replace the loan with a new one, if your credit and equity still allow it.

If keeping the home isn't realistic

  • Sell the home before the auction — pay off the loan and walk away with your equity. With time, a traditional listing may net the most; when the clock is tight, a cash sale offers speed and certainty.
  • Short sale — if you owe more than the home is worth, the lender agrees to accept a payoff for less than the full balance.
  • Deed in lieu of foreclosure — you voluntarily hand the deed back to the lender. Usually a last resort, and it still affects your credit.
  • Bankruptcy — filing triggers an automatic stay that pauses the sale. This is a serious legal step; talk to a bankruptcy attorney before considering it.

Why Selling Before the Auction Is Often the Smartest Move

When a home goes all the way to a trustee's sale, the homeowner usually walks away with nothing — even when there was real equity in the property. Selling beforehand flips that outcome:

  • You keep your equity. The sale pays off the loan and liens, and the rest is yours.
  • You protect your credit. A completed sale stops the foreclosure, so it never lands on your credit report — where it could otherwise linger for up to seven years.
  • You keep control. You choose the closing date and move on your terms rather than the lender's.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have before I lose my house to foreclosure in California?

California foreclosure is a process, not an instant event. After a Notice of Default is recorded, there's a minimum 90-day waiting period before the lender can set a sale date, and the Notice of Trustee's Sale must give at least 21 more days before the auction. That's a minimum of roughly 111 days from the Notice of Default — and in practice it's often longer. You can act at any point during that window.

Can I sell my house if I've already received a Notice of Default?

Yes. A Notice of Default doesn't transfer ownership — you still own the home and can sell it any time before the trustee's sale. Selling lets you pay off the loan and keep your remaining equity instead of losing it at auction. A cash sale can often close inside the foreclosure timeline.

Will selling my house stop the foreclosure from hurting my credit?

A completed sale before the trustee's sale pays off the loan and stops the foreclosure, so a completed foreclosure never appears on your credit report. A finished foreclosure can affect your credit for up to seven years, so selling beforehand makes a real difference.

Can I stop foreclosure if I'm underwater on my mortgage?

Possibly. If you owe more than the home is worth, a short sale — where the lender agrees to accept less than the full balance — may be an option. In a California non-judicial foreclosure, lenders generally can't pursue you for the remaining shortfall after the sale, but you should confirm your specific situation with an attorney.

Is a loan modification better than selling my house?

It depends on whether keeping the home is realistic long-term. If your hardship was temporary and you can afford the payments going forward, a loan modification or reinstatement is often the better path, and a HUD-approved housing counselor can help for free. If the payments simply aren't sustainable, selling before the auction usually protects you far better than losing the home to foreclosure.
