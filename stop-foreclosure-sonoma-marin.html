Facing Foreclosure in Sonoma or Marin County? You Have More Options Than You Think

A missed-payment letter or a Notice of Default in the mail is frightening — but it does not mean you've already lost your home. In California, foreclosure follows a defined legal timeline, and that timeline gives you a real window to act. The single most important thing to understand is this: if your home has equity, selling it before the auction lets you pay off what you owe, walk away with your remaining money, and avoid a completed foreclosure on your record.

We're Evan and Alex — local, licensed buyers based right here in Sonoma County. We've helped homeowners across both counties navigate exactly this situation. There's no judgment here, and no pressure. Just honest help understanding where you stand and what your best move actually is.

How Foreclosure Works in California — and Where Your Window Is

California uses mostly non-judicial foreclosure, which moves through a set sequence of steps. Knowing which step you're on tells you roughly how much time you have:

1. You fall behind on payments

Under federal rules, most lenders wait until you're around 120 days behind before they can start the formal foreclosure process. This is often the best time to act — while you have the most time.

2. Notice of Default (NOD) is recorded

Your lender records a Notice of Default with the county recorder. It's public record, and it starts the clock. From here, there's a minimum 90-day waiting period before the next step.

3. Reinstatement period

During that period you can usually "reinstate" the loan by paying the past-due amount plus fees — up until 5 business days before the sale. You can also sell the home during this entire window.

4. Notice of Trustee's Sale (NOS)

After the 90 days, the trustee can record and publish a Notice of Sale, setting an auction date at least 21 days out.

5. The trustee's sale (auction)

Your home is sold to the highest bidder. In a California non-judicial foreclosure, there is generally no right to buy it back after the sale — so this is the deadline that matters most.

The takeaway: from the Notice of Default, you typically have somewhere around three to four months before the auction — and you can sell the home at any point before that sale happens. Wait until the auction, and any equity you had is usually gone.

Timelines vary, and you may have more options than selling. Your servicer may offer a loan modification or forbearance, and a HUD-approved housing counselor can review your situation for free. We're glad to point you to those resources — even if it means you don't sell to us.

Why Selling Before the Auction Protects You

When a home goes all the way to a trustee's sale, the homeowner usually walks away with nothing — even if there was equity in the property. Selling beforehand changes that outcome in three important ways:

  • You keep your equity. The sale pays off your loan and any liens, and the remaining proceeds are yours — instead of being lost at auction.
  • You protect your credit. A completed sale stops the foreclosure, so a foreclosure never lands on your credit report. A finished foreclosure can affect your credit for up to seven years.
  • You stay in control. You choose the closing date and move on your own terms, rather than being forced out on the lender's schedule.

Your Options When You're Behind on Payments

Selling isn't the only path, and we'll always tell you honestly which one fits your situation:

  • Reinstate the loan — catch up the past-due balance if you're able to. Best if the hardship was temporary.
  • Loan modification or forbearance — work with your servicer to change the terms. The right choice if you can afford the home long-term.
  • Sell before the auction for cash — the fastest, most certain way to protect your equity and credit. This is where we come in.
  • List it on the market with us — if you have enough time before the sale and the home would net more on the MLS, we're licensed agents and we'll tell you so honestly.
  • Short sale — if you owe more than the home is worth, we can help negotiate a payoff with your lender for less than the full balance.

Foreclosure FAQs — Sonoma & Marin County

Can I still sell my house after I've received a Notice of Default in California?

Yes. A Notice of Default does not mean you've lost the home. In California you can sell at any point before the trustee's sale, and you can typically reinstate the loan up until 5 business days before that sale. Selling before the auction lets you pay off what you owe and keep your remaining equity instead of losing it at foreclosure.

How fast can you close if my foreclosure auction date is coming up?

We can close in as little as 7 days when the title is clear. If you have a trustee's sale date set, tell us right away — we coordinate directly with your lender, the trustee, and a local title company to get the payoff done before the sale.

What if I owe more on my mortgage than the house is worth?

You may still have options. If you're underwater, we can help pursue a short sale by negotiating with your lender to accept a payoff for less than the full balance. As licensed California agents, we have experience working through these situations.

Will selling my house stop the foreclosure and protect my credit?

A completed sale before the trustee's sale pays off the loan and stops the foreclosure, so a completed foreclosure never lands on your credit report. That's a meaningful difference — a foreclosure can affect your credit for up to seven years.

Are there any fees, commissions, or costs to sell to you?

No. Zero fees, zero commissions, and we cover normal closing costs. The cash offer we make is the amount that goes toward your loan payoff and your equity — there are no surprise deductions at the closing table.

Should I try for a loan modification instead of selling?

It depends on your situation, and we'll be honest with you. If you can realistically afford the home long-term, a loan modification or reinstatement may be your best path, and a HUD-approved housing counselor can help for free. If keeping the home isn't realistic, selling before the auction almost always protects you better than losing it at foreclosure.
